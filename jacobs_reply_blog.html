A Response from the Dev: Thank You, and Here's What's Changing

by Jacob (xperttheef) — Owner & Developer of Astro Social / Logic Media



————————————————————————

First of all, I want to say thank you. Genuinely. Not a polite, hollow "thanks for the feedback" kind of thank you — a real one. Getting a detailed, thoughtful, clearly well-researched blog from one of the most active users on your platform — someone who is also on your admin team, who has promoted your site across multiple communities, and who still chose to write publicly about the things that need fixing — that's not an attack. That's a gift. Every developer building something from the ground up should be so lucky to have someone in their corner who cares enough to write thousands of words about how to make it better.

I read every single word of that blog, and I want to respond to it directly, openly, and honestly — point by point — the way I believe a developer should respond to his own community. No dodging, no deflecting, no corporate non-answers.

Who I Am and Why I Built This

For those of you reading this from Paint who may not know me well — or who only know me by reputation — let me reintroduce myself. My name is Jacob, and I go by xperttheef online. I've been a member of the Paint community for almost five years now. I've watched it grow, watched it change, watched people come and go. I've been through the drama, the good times, the debates, and all the weird little moments that make a community feel like a community. Paint is home in a lot of ways, and that's not something I take lightly.

I built Astro Social because I believed — and still believe — that there's room for something new. Not a replacement for Paint, not a knockoff, not a spite project. Something that can stand on its own alongside platforms like misdew.com, 3DsPaint.com, and SocialCu.be. Each of those platforms has its own strengths, its own community, its own identity. Astro Social is my attempt to add something to that ecosystem, not to tear anything down.

When I started building this platform, my vision was simple: create a space that respects its users, gives them real tools for expression, and doesn't shy away from the hard conversations about what a community platform should look like in 2026. That vision hasn't changed. But a vision without execution is just a daydream, and the blog I'm responding to today is a reality check on the execution side. A welcome one.

The Word Filter — You're Right, and I'm Fixing It

Let me just say it plainly: the word filter, as it currently exists, is broken. Not broken in the sense that it doesn't work — it works [i]too well[/i], and in all the wrong ways. When one of my admins can't type the word "minutes" in a normal conversation without getting censored, that's not content moderation. That's a bug wearing a moderator's hat.

I want to explain how we got here, because I think context matters. When blueturtle started flooding the platform with alt accounts and inflammatory content, the admin team — rightfully — wanted tools to fight back. One of those tools was the word filter. The intention was to keep the worst of the worst out of the main Feed area. The problem is that the filter was built with a broad brush when it needed a scalpel. Words were added to the banned list reactively, in the heat of dealing with spam and harassment, without enough thought given to how those words function in everyday, normal conversation.

The word "Nazi," for example. As was correctly pointed out in the blog, blueturtle himself doesn't even use that word very often. So banning it does almost nothing to combat his behavior, while simultaneously preventing legitimate historical, political, and educational conversations from happening on the platform. That's a net negative. That's moderation working against its own community.

And the fact that the filter apparently extends beyond the main Feed into the Chat section — when my intention was only ever to moderate the Feed more strictly — tells me that the implementation needs a serious overhaul, not just a tweak.

Here's what I'm committing to:

- A full audit of the banned words list. Every single word on that list is going to be reviewed. Words with clear, legitimate everyday uses — like "minutes," like "Nazi" in historical context, like dozens of others that probably shouldn't be there — will be removed or moved to context-specific restrictions.

- Section-specific filtering. The main Feed can have tighter moderation. That's fine. But the Chat section, group discussions, blogs, and DMs should have significantly more freedom. Different spaces on the platform serve different purposes, and the filter should reflect that.

- Transparency about what's filtered and why. If a word is on the restricted list, users — especially admins — should be able to know that and understand the reasoning. Invisible censorship breeds frustration and distrust, and that's the opposite of what I want here.

The comparison to the "saltwater" situation on Paint is completely fair, and honestly, it stings a little — because I know how frustrating that was for people over there, and I don't want to be repeating someone else's mistakes on my own platform. Point taken. Loud and clear.

Geopolitics, Auto-Flagging, and the Israel Poll

This one deserves a longer conversation, because it touches on something I care deeply about: what kind of platform Astro Social is going to be.

A legitimate poll — asking people's opinions about nuclear weapons policy in West Asia — was auto-flagged as "propaganda" and deleted because it contained the word "Israel." I want to be very clear about something: that is not the kind of platform I'm building. You should be able to name a country on Astro Social. You should be able to discuss foreign policy, geopolitics, current events, and yes, even controversial topics — without an algorithm deciding that your opinion is propaganda before a single human being has even read it.

Now, I understand [i]why[/i] auto-flagging systems exist. When you're a small platform dealing with a relentless flood of alt accounts and bad-faith actors, automation feels like a lifeline. You can't have an admin reviewing every single post in real time, especially when you're getting hit with waves of spam. But automation without nuance is just censorship with plausible deniability, and I refuse to let Astro Social become that.

The auto-flagging rules around politically sensitive keywords are going to be completely reworked. Here's my approach going forward:

- Geographic and political terms will not be auto-flagged. Country names, leader names, political party names — these are basic vocabulary for anyone discussing current events. Flagging them is absurd, and it stops now.

- Auto-flagging will target behavior patterns, not individual words. Spam looks like spam — rapid-fire posting, copy-paste floods, brand-new accounts posting inflammatory content within seconds of creation. Those are the patterns the system should be catching, not a longtime user submitting a thoughtful poll.

- Flagged content will be held for review, not auto-deleted. If the system flags something, it should go into a moderation queue where an actual human — one of our admins — can look at it and make a judgment call. Immediate deletion with no review is unacceptable for legitimate content.

I've said publicly that I believe in freedom of speech, and I meant it. That doesn't mean Astro Social is a free-for-all with no rules — it means that the rules should target genuinely harmful behavior (threats, harassment, targeted hate, spam), not political opinions, historical discussions, or the mere mention of a country's name. There's a massive difference between moderating [i]behavior[/i] and moderating [i]ideas[/i], and I intend to stay firmly on the right side of that line.

As for the broader question of which sections of Astro Social are "free speech" friendly and which are more moderated — that's a fair point that needs clarity. Here's my thinking: the main Feed is the front page of the platform. It's what new users see first. I want it to be welcoming and relatively clean. But groups, channels, blogs, and DMs? Those are spaces where people should be able to have real conversations, including uncomfortable ones. I'll be publishing clearer guidelines about this so that every user knows what to expect and where.

Security and the Alt Problem

I know the blog addressed this as well, and while the proposed security measure is something I'm still evaluating on the technical side, I want to acknowledge the core point: the alt spam problem is real, it's exhausting, and it's unsustainable.

Every admin on this platform — including the author of that blog — has spent way too many hours dealing with blueturtle's alt accounts. That's time and energy that should be going toward building the community, creating content, and making the platform better. Instead, it's going toward an endless game of whack-a-mole, and I understand the frustration.

I'm not going to pretend I have the perfect solution today, but I am going to be transparent about the fact that this is a top priority. I'm looking at multiple approaches — including the one that was proposed — and the goal is to implement something effective as soon as possible. A platform that can't control who creates accounts on it is a platform that will never grow, and I hear that message loud and clear.

What I will say is this: the admin team has done incredible work handling this problem under extremely difficult circumstances, and I don't want that to go unrecognized. The fact that Astro Social is still functional, still growing, and still has active users posting real content is a testament to the people who have been showing up every single day to keep it running. That includes the person who wrote the blog I'm responding to.

The Minor Pet Peeves — They're Not Minor to Me

The blog listed several "minor pet peeves," but I want to address them because I don't think they're minor at all. They're quality-of-life issues, and quality of life is what separates a platform people [i]tolerate[/i] from a platform people [i]love[/i].

Profile Comments: You're right. Being able to leave comments on profiles is a feature that Paint does well, and it adds a social layer that Astro Social is currently missing. It's one of those features that seems small but actually drives a ton of casual interaction. It's on the roadmap now.

The DM System: I hear this. The current DM system works, but "works" isn't the same as "works well." The reply system especially needs attention. I've used both Paint's and SocialCu.be's DM systems, and I agree that there are things we can learn from both. SocialCu.be's approach in particular is clean and intuitive. Rebuilding the DM experience is going to be a priority — it's one of the most personal parts of any social platform, and it needs to feel right.

Blog Formatting: If the person who writes the most blogs on the platform is having formatting issues, that's a problem with the platform, not with the person. Full stop. I need to make the blog editor more forgiving and more intuitive. Whether it's spacing issues, image handling, or whatever caused the original version of [i]this very blog[/i] to fail to post on Astro Social — that's unacceptable. A platform that has a blog feature should make it easy to blog. I'm going to dig into this and fix it.

Polls Section: Having a dedicated section for polls, rather than burying them in the main Feed, makes total sense. Polls are a unique type of content — they're interactive, they invite participation, and they deserve their own space where people can browse and engage with them. This is going on the to-do list.

Groups — Feed vs. Chat Confusion: Honestly? This is fair criticism. If the difference between a group's Feed and a group's Chat isn't obvious to an admin who uses the platform every day, then the distinction probably isn't clear enough — or maybe isn't necessary at all. I'm going to re-evaluate how groups work and see if there's a simpler, more intuitive structure. The idea of group pages similar to what Paint offers is something I'm very open to exploring.

Profile Pages: These are coming. A dedicated, customizable profile page where users can express themselves is fundamental to any social platform. It's not a question of "if," it's a question of "when," and I'm pushing to make it sooner rather than later.

Editing Posts and Blogs: Yes. Absolutely yes. The ability to edit your own content after posting it is basic functionality that should have been there from day one. I know this is an issue on other platforms too, but that doesn't make it acceptable here. This is near the top of the priority list.

Direct Links to Content: Being able to link directly to a specific blog, group, post, or piece of content is important — not just for convenience, but for the health of the platform. It's how content gets shared, referenced, and discovered. When someone writes a blog and wants to link to a previous post or a specific group discussion, they should be able to do that. This is going to be implemented.
